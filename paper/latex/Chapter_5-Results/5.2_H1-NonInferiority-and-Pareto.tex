\section{H1: Non-Inferiority and the Performance--Cost Frontier}
\label{sec:h1}

Contrary to the intuition that an agent which remembers more of its past should solve
more of its future, \textbf{no forgetting policy is distinguishable from full-memory
accumulation on the resolution proxy}, and the policy that carries the least memory is
the one that resolves the most tasks. Because retrieval is held constant across all six
conditions, this is not a confound of \emph{how} memory is searched but a direct
consequence of \emph{what} each policy chooses to keep --- which licenses reading the
comparison as a clean effect of the forgetting policy itself.

\subsection{The primary test: no policy beats full memory}

The pre-registered primary analysis is a Wilcoxon signed-rank test on $N=8$
sequence-level means, comparing each of the five other policies against
\texttt{full\_memory}, with Holm correction across the five contrasts
(the pre-registered primary test; see Section~\ref{sec:metrics}). The result is uniform:
\textbf{every contrast is non-significant after correction}, and every bootstrap
confidence interval on the paired difference crosses zero (Table~\ref{tab:main-results}).
Proactive forgetting is, on this evidence, \emph{non-inferior} to full-memory
accumulation. The closest any contrast comes to significance is \texttt{no\_memory}
versus \texttt{full\_memory} ($p=0.109$ uncorrected, large effect $r_{rb}=-0.667$) --- a
directional hint that \emph{some} memory beats none, but one that does not survive as a
significant difference.

\subsection{The null is bounded and powered, not a bare ``no difference''}

A non-significant result is only informative if the test could have detected an effect
worth caring about. Two sensitivity analyses establish that it could, and bound what the
null actually rules out. We report these in the estimation spirit advocated by
\cite{cumming2014} and \cite{wasserstein2019} --- as intervals and detectable-effect
sizes, not as a second significance verdict.

\emph{First, the memory benefit is bounded.} The aggregate advantage of full memory over
no memory is small but real: a mean difference of \textbf{+2.40 percentage points}
(bootstrap 95\% CI $[+0.47,\ +4.35]$ pp), positive in 6 of 8 sequences. Memory helps a
little; we can rule out an aggregate benefit larger than roughly 4.4 pp.

\emph{Second, the test is well powered for any effect that size or larger.} Under the
observed sequence-level noise, the $N=8$ Wilcoxon design has power 0.29 to detect a 2 pp
per-sequence difference, 0.68 at 3 pp, and \textbf{1.00 at 4 pp or more}. Two readings
follow. The policy-null is \emph{meaningful}: any forgetting policy that cost (or saved)
4 pp or more relative to full memory would have been detected, and none did. And the
$\approx$2.4 pp memory benefit sits \emph{below} the pre-registered test's resolution ---
which is precisely why the primary Wilcoxon is non-significant while the bootstrap
interval excludes zero. We therefore report the Wilcoxon result as primary and treat the
bootstrap and power analyses as a sensitivity bound on a small effect, rather than
re-labelling the effect as significant under a more favourable test.

\emph{Third, per task the memory effect is within the agent's own noise.} Switching
memory on or off flips 19.1\% of individual task outcomes (71 rescued, 59 broken), but
the within-policy, seed-to-seed flip rate is already 17--20\% (e.g.\ \texttt{no\_memory}
19.8\%, \texttt{full\_memory} 18.5\%). Per-task memory churn is thus indistinguishable
from the agent's stochastic noise floor; 80.9\% of tasks are memory-invariant
(23\% solved under both regimes --- a capability ceiling --- and 58\% solved under
neither --- too hard regardless). The correct reading is multi-scale: a \emph{small,
systematic} sequence-level tilt toward memory (+2.4 pp) riding on top of \emph{large}
per-task noise. This also validates the design --- a single-seed comparison would have
been uninterpretable.

\subsection{The two-axis frontier: a compute null and a footprint win}

The efficiency contribution (H1b) is best read on two separate axes, because forgetting
behaves very differently on each.

\paragraph{Compute: a null.}
On total token cost (Figure~\ref{fig:pareto-compute}) the six policies are
\emph{indistinguishable}. Per-policy gaps are on the order of 0.2--0.5M tokens against a
paired standard deviation of $\approx$2.2M --- so far inside the noise that
\texttt{full\_memory} actually consumed \emph{more} tokens than \texttt{recency\_prune}
in 16 of 24 paired cells. The reason is structural: cost is dominated by the agent loop,
not by memory. Each task runs up to 20 ReAct steps whose growing conversation is resent
every step, so the prompt alone averages $\approx$151K tokens per task; the memory
payload is $\approx$0.4--1\% of that, and the tool-call budget ($\approx$21.5/task) is
flat across policies. Pruning a few hundred tokens of memory cannot move a six-million-token
bill. The compute frontier is therefore a tangle of overlapping confidence ellipses, not
a ranking.

\paragraph{Footprint: where forgetting wins.}
The picture inverts on the memory-footprint axis (Figure~\ref{fig:pareto-footprint}),
the quantity that governs storage and retrieval-index growth over a long-running agent's
lifetime. Here \texttt{full\_memory} carries $\approx$2{,}986 tokens/task and is
\emph{Pareto-dominated}: \texttt{recency\_prune} reaches a \emph{higher} resolution rate
(0.330 vs 0.311) while carrying $\approx$4$\times$ less memory ($\approx$698 tokens), and
\texttt{no\_memory} anchors the zero-footprint corner. The Pareto-optimal set is the
light-footprint policies; full accumulation buys nothing on outcomes that the pruners do
not also deliver, while paying a $4\times$ footprint premium.

\paragraph{The combined claim.}
Non-inferior on the value axis, indistinguishable on compute, and dominant on footprint:
aggressive forgetting matches full-memory accumulation on task resolution at roughly a
quarter of the memory footprint and no compute penalty. ``Not every task needs memory''
begins to pay off here --- and the rest of the chapter explains \emph{why} the value-axis
gap is so small that throwing memory away costs nothing.
