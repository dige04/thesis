\section{Experimental Setup and Descriptive Statistics}

The evaluation rests on a single balanced matrix of \textbf{144 controlled runs}:
6 memory policies $\times$ 8 official SWE-Bench-CL sequences $\times$ 3 seeds, with
24 runs per policy and 48 runs per seed. Retrieval scoring is held constant across all
six conditions (pure cosine, identical $k$ and context budget), so the only factor that
varies between conditions is \emph{what each policy chooses to keep or forget}. A single
frozen model, \texttt{deepseek-v4-flash}, serves every model role (the coding agent, the
memory-type classifier, reflection, and CLS consolidation); because the model is a fixed
factor across all conditions, between-policy contrasts are valid even though absolute
resolution rates are not comparable to public SWE-Bench leaderboards. The full slate of
model, embedder, cost-metric, and host substitutions is disclosed in
Section~\ref{sec:deviations}.

The matrix is complete and clean. Across \textbf{4{,}675} task rows there are no
zero-task units, no container or image-pull failures, and no retrieval-index corruption
that survived into the recorded outcomes; \textbf{1{,}472} tasks resolve, for an overall
resolution rate of \textbf{31.5\%}. All inferential statistics in the sections that
follow take the \emph{sequence} as the unit of analysis ($N=8$ sequence-level means per
policy), never the individual task --- a deliberate guard against pseudo-replication.

\paragraph{Descriptive picture.}
Table~\ref{tab:descriptives} reports per-policy means over the eight sequences on the
three outcome axes the thesis cares about: resolution rate (the CL-F1 proxy; see the
caveat below), memory footprint carried per task, and total token cost per run. Two
features matter before any test is run. First, the policies are tightly bunched on
resolution rate --- a spread of roughly four percentage points separates the best pruner
(\texttt{recency\_prune}, 0.330) from the no-memory baseline (0.287), with
\texttt{full\_memory} (0.311) sitting inside that band rather than on top of it. Second,
the policies differ by nearly $4\times$ on footprint: \texttt{full\_memory} carries
$\approx$2{,}986 tokens of memory per task while the pruning policies carry
$\approx$666--897 and \texttt{no\_memory} carries none. The tool-call budget
($\approx$21.5 calls/task) is essentially identical across all six conditions, a first
hint --- developed in Section~\ref{sec:h4} --- that the memory policy does not change how
the agent behaves step to step.

\paragraph{Headroom for a memory effect.}
At an overall resolution rate of 31.5\% the experiment sits well clear of both the floor
(no sequence is at 0\%) and the ceiling (none is saturated near 100\%). This matters: if
the agent solved almost everything or almost nothing regardless of memory, no
between-policy contrast would be interpretable, because there would be no outcomes left
for memory to move. The observed mid-range rate leaves genuine room for a memory effect
to register --- which makes the near-null results of the following sections a finding
about memory, not an artefact of a saturated benchmark.

\paragraph{What ``CL-F1'' means here.}
Throughout this chapter the reported CL-F1 equals the per-condition resolution rate
($\text{mean\_cl\_f1} \equiv \text{mean\_resolved\_rate}$); the full anchor-probe
instrumentation that would isolate the CL-specific \emph{stability} dimension was not run
at matrix scale. The stability dimension is instead addressed through a targeted
construct-validity sub-study (Section~\ref{sec:h5}) and disclosed in Threats to Validity
(Section~\ref{sec:threats}). Readers should therefore read CL-F1 in this thesis as a
resolution-rate proxy, not as a forgetting-curve measurement.
