\section{The Six Memory Policies}
\label{sec:policies}

The six conditions are best read as a \emph{dose ladder} of retention. No Memory is the
zero dose --- nothing is carried across tasks; Full Memory is the maximum dose --- nothing
is ever discarded; and the four interior policies sit between them, each forgetting a
different subset once the store fills. The ladder operationalises ``not every task needs
memory'' as a controlled gradient: rather than asking whether memory helps in the
abstract, it varies how much, and which, experience the agent keeps.

The two boundary baselines bracket the comparison. No Memory establishes the floor against
which any benefit of remembering is measured; Full Memory establishes the ceiling and the
accumulation cost that proactive forgetting must either match (the non-inferiority
question) or beat on footprint. The four interior policies --- Random Prune, Recency
Prune, Type-Aware Decay, and CLS Consolidation --- differ \emph{only} in what they forget
when the store exceeds the budget; they share an identical retrieval mechanism with each
other and with the baselines. The binding budget is a cap of ten records (amendment A1),
which is smaller than the shortest sequence, so eviction genuinely fires for all four
interior policies; No Memory has nothing to cap, and Full Memory ignores the cap by design.

Every condition runs the same per-task loop --- retrieve, solve, reflect, classify, and
then \emph{maintain} the store --- and the policies diverge at exactly one step, the
maintenance step where forgetting fires (Figure~\ref{fig:task-lifecycle}). Holding the rest
of the loop identical is what makes the dose ladder a clean single-factor manipulation.
Table~\ref{tab:policies} summarises the six.

  \begin{tabular}{lll}

  \end{tabular}
\end{table}
