\section{Positioning: The Retrieval-Fixed, Controlled-Comparison Gap}

The two preceding sections converge on a single unoccupied position. Multi-policy forgetting
work, exemplified by Alqithami's six-policy sweep~\cite{alqithami2025}, lives in the
conversational domain and does not hold retrieval constant, so its policy effects are
entangled with search and not cleanly attributable. Coding-agent memory systems do operate
in the right domain but vary retrieval granularity alongside storage and report superiority
rather than non-inferiority, so their gains likewise cannot be ascribed to the storage
decision alone. And a third line --- adaptive, ``when to retrieve'' work --- shows that
retrieval is not always worth doing: routing or skipping retrieval by query difficulty can
help, and indiscriminate retrieval can actively hurt, which is the agent-level echo of the
experience-following risk~\cite{xiong2025}. That line motivates the thesis's interdependence
analysis: if only a minority of tasks structurally depend on a predecessor, then for most
tasks there is nothing to retrieve and ``not every task needs memory'' is not a slogan but a
measurable property of the benchmark (Figure~\ref{fig:interdependence}).

In contrast, the wedge this thesis occupies is coding agents on SWE-Bench-CL with
\emph{retrieval held as a fixed control} --- pure cosine similarity, identical across all
six policies --- so that the storage and forgetting policy is the only independent variable.
The question is posed as non-inferiority rather than superiority: not ``does memory help?''
but ``can an agent forget aggressively and match full accumulation on resolution while
carrying a smaller footprint?'' To our knowledge this combination --- coding domain,
retrieval fixed, storage isolated, non-inferiority framing, two cost axes --- is not
occupied by any prior work, and supplying it is the contribution of the chapters that
follow.
